Now. Before we talk about where to put your $100 —

Most beginners think the first $100 is about
finding the perfect investment.
Like there's some magical stock or crypto you just need to pick.

Your first $100 isn't about picking winners.

Turning yourself into someone who invests

The biggest difference between people who build wealth

[FRAMEWORK REVEAL — 2:30]
I call it the Three Bucket System.
Before you invest a single dollar,
you need to know which bucket your money belongs in.
Bucket One: Safety.
Bucket Two: High-Interest Debt.
Bucket Three: Actual Investing.
Let's go through each one.

[BUCKET 1 — 3:00]
Bucket One. Safety.
Ask yourself one question before you invest anything.
If an unexpected expense hits tomorrow —
will you need this money back immediately?
If yes — investing is not step one.
The Federal Reserve found that when faced
with a $400 emergency expense,
nearly 40% of adults couldn't cover it with cash.
If a surprise bill forces you to sell your investment
at the worst possible moment —
investing didn't help you. It hurt you.
Here's what most people don't realize.
Putting $100 into a basic savings account isn't a boring move.
It's investing in your ability to stay invested later.
Because when financial stress hits,
your brain doesn't calmly analyze long-term returns.
It says: I need cash now.
Psychologists call this loss aversion.
We feel losses more intensely than gains.
That's what causes beginners to panic sell
at exactly the wrong moment.
Even a small emergency buffer prevents that.

[BUCKET 2 — 4:30]
Bucket Two. High-Interest Debt.
If you're carrying credit card debt —
paying it down might be your best investment right now.
Here's why.
The average credit card interest rate in the US is now over 20%.
Paying off that debt is like earning a guaranteed 20% return.
The stock market does not guarantee anything close to that.
So if you have high-interest debt, your first $100 might go there.
Not because investing is bad —
but because you're trying to fill a bucket
that currently has a hole in it.

[BUCKET 3 — 5:30]
Bucket Three. Actual Investing.
Now. Let's say you have a small buffer.
No urgent high-interest debt.
And you can leave this money alone.
Now we invest.
And I'm going to be direct.
Your first $100 should not go into individual stocks.
Not meme coins. Not penny stocks. Not crypto.
Could you get lucky? Sure.
But that's not investing. That's lottery behavior.
The smart approach is much simpler.
Buy the entire market. At a low cost.
For most beginners, that means one of these three:
A total market index fund.
An S&P 500 index fund.
Or a target-date index fund.
Because even professional fund managers
struggle to beat the market consistently.
If professionals can't beat it —
a beginner probably won't do it with a $100 trade either.

[EXACT STEPS — 6:30]
Here's exactly what doing this looks like.
Step one. Choose the account.
Use the most tax-efficient account available to you.
In most countries that means a retirement account first,
then a regular brokerage account.
Step two. Choose one fund.
Don't overcomplicate this.
Pick one diversified index fund.
Total market, S&P 500, or a target-date fund. That's it.
Step three. Invest the $100.
Most brokerages now offer fractional shares —
meaning you can invest even if a full share costs more than $100.
Buy the fund. You're invested.
Step four — and this is the most important step.
Automate your next contribution.
The first $100 barely matters. The habit does.
Set up an automatic transfer.
$10 a week. $25 a week. Whatever you can.
Automation beats motivation every single time.

[COMPOUND MATH — 7:30]
Here's the math most people never see.
If you invest $100 once and never add another dollar —
at 7% annual growth over 30 years —
that becomes about $760.
Not life-changing.
But watch what happens when you build the habit.
If you invest just $25 per week
for 30 years at the same 7% return —
you end up with roughly $120,000.
Same market. Same return.
The only difference — consistency.
