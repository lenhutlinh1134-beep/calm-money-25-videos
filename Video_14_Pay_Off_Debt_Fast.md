Line up all your debts from smallest to largest.
Ignore interest rates.

Just smallest to largest.
Now, you make minimum payments on all of them.

On the smallest one, you attack it with everything.
Pay it off fast.
Delete it.

Minimum payments on the others.

Psychological win. Momentum spike.
Now attack the $2,500.
Four more months. Boom. Second win.
Now the momentum carries you.

[THE AVALANCHE METHOD — 4:00]

Now let's look at Avalanche.
Same idea, different order.
Instead of smallest to largest,
you order by interest rate.
Highest interest first.
You make minimum payments on everything.
But you attack the highest interest debt first.
Why?
Because mathematically, it costs you the most money.
High interest debt is bleeding you.
A 25% APR credit card is destroying your life.
An Avalanche attack focuses on the worst offender.
Real example. Same five debts.

Credit card 1: $800 at 24% APR
Credit card 2: $2,500 at 22% APR
Medical debt: $3,500 at 18% APR
Student loan: $8,000 at 6% APR
Car payment: $12,000 at 4% APR
You attack the 24% card first.
Mathematically, this saves the most money.
You stop the bleeding.
The other debts accumulate interest slowly.
But you're focusing firepower on the worst one.

Let me show you the actual math.
$10,000 credit card debt at 25% APR.
If you make minimum payments only,
you pay approximately $18,000 in interest.
You end up paying $28,000 for $10,000 of debt.
Nearly triple.
Same $10,000 debt at 7% APR.
You pay approximately $3,000 in interest.
You end up paying $13,000 for $10,000 of debt.
That's a $15,000 difference in interest alone.
Just from interest rate.
So mathematically, Avalanche saves more money
because you eliminate the high-interest debt first.
But here's the psychological part.
If the motivation isn't there,
you never stick with Avalanche long enough
to see the savings.
You give up at month 4 because you feel no progress.
And giving up means you pay all $28,000 of interest
instead of the savings Avalanche promises.

Avalanche saves more money mathematically.
Snowball works better psychologically.

Ask yourself: Am I disciplined and patient?

You can stay motivated for 18 months
even if early progress looks slow.
You're motivated by math, not wins.
Ask yourself: Do I need visible progress?

Most people are the second type.
Most people need momentum.
So Snowball is the answer for most.

You get momentum plus mathematical savings.
