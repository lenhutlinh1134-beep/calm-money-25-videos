**SECTION 1: PRICING PSYCHOLOGY & THE DATA (0:30–2:15)**

Pricing is where most indie developers leave 90% of their money on the table.

Either they underprice—no revenue per customer, never hit profitability. Or they overprice—no one buys.

Here's the data-driven approach that works.

Here's a counterintuitive fact: Higher prices often convert better than lower prices.

Why? Because price signals quality. Lower prices attract price-sensitive customers—bad reviews, high churn. Higher prices attract serious customers who stay longer and require less support.

Example from 2025 indie SaaS data:

Product A at $9 per month: 40 signups, 5 conversions. That's 12.5% conversion rate. 3-month churn.

Product B, identical product, $39 per month: 8 signups, 4 conversions. That's 50% conversion rate. 18-month churn.

Product B made more money: $156 versus $135. With fewer customers and less support burden.

This is the core insight. Price isn't just revenue. It's a quality signal.

---

**SECTION 2: THE TIERED PRICING MODEL (2:15–4:45)**

Most successful Claude-powered products use three tiers.

Tier 1 Starter: $19 per month. 100 API calls per month. Basic support. For hobbyists and freelancers trying it out. Conversion: 15-20% of visitors. Lifetime Value: about $190—they stay 10 months average.

Tier 2 Professional: $49 per month. Unlimited API calls. Priority support. Advanced features. For small businesses and working freelancers. Conversion: 50-60% of paying customers land here. Lifetime Value: about $735—they stay 15 months average.

Tier 3 Business: $149 per month. Unlimited API calls. Dedicated support. API access. White label option. For agencies and growing companies. Conversion: 15-20% of paying customers. Lifetime Value: about $2,235—they stay 15 months average.

Why this structure works: The $19 tier is your "free trial alternative." It anchors the price. People feel like they're getting value at any tier.

The $49 tier is your profit center. 60% of customers pay this. It's high enough to be sustainable, low enough to feel reasonable.

The $149 tier is your upside. Not everyone pays it, but the ones who do double your revenue from that customer.

Pricing psychology: Each tier is 2.5 to 3 times the previous. This feels like a jump in value, not just incrementally more money.

---

**SECTION 3: AVOIDING MISTAKES & TESTING PRICING (4:45–7:00)**

Here are the biggest pricing mistakes:

Mistake 1: Too many tiers. Five-plus tiers confuse customers. Stick to three.

Mistake 2: Feature differentiation is too small. Tier 1 and Tier 2 need clearly different value. Tier 1 is limited usage. Tier 2 is unlimited plus advanced features. Tier 3 is white label plus API access.

Mistake 3: Pricing is too similar to competitors. You're not competing on price. You're competing on speed of delivery, specific use case, customer service. Price your value, not the market.

Mistake 4: No free tier or trial. Offer a 7 to 14-day free trial for all tiers. This removes risk for customers. Higher conversion than a free forever tier.

Now, how to test pricing:

Step 1: Launch with your best guess. Don't overthink. $19/$49/$149 is the starting point. You can change it anytime.

Step 2: Track what happens. First 20 customers—what tier did they choose? If 80% choose Tier 1, you're too expensive OR Tier 1 is the right price. If 80% choose Tier 3, you're too cheap. If the split is 20/60/20, you nailed it.

Step 3: Adjust after 20 customers. If most choose lowest tier, lower all prices by 20%. If most choose highest tier, raise all prices by 30%. If split is balanced, keep it.

Step 4: Iterate quarterly. Each quarter, test a 10-15% price increase. Small increases rarely hurt conversion and increase revenue significantly.

Example: Raise $49 to $54 to $59 to $64 over 12 months.

---

**SECTION 4: ANNUAL PRICING & RAISING PRICES (7:00–8:30)**

Once you have monthly customers, offer annual pricing.

Monthly: $49 per month equals $588 per year.

Annual: $490 per year. That's a 17% discount.

This works because 20-30% of customers take it. You get cash upfront, which helps cash flow. Churn drops—they already paid for a year.

The trick: Make the discount feel real but not so big that monthly customers feel punished.

When to raise prices: Raise prices when either happens first. One: 80% of customers choose your highest tier—you're underpricing. Two: 12 months have passed and you have 30-plus customers—you're sustainable.

How to raise: Announce 30 days in advance. Current customers keep the old price—grandfather them. New customers at new price. Communicate the value: new features, better support.

Example raise:

Old pricing: $19/$49/$149.

New pricing: $29/$69/$199. That's a 33% increase.

Announcement: "Adding [new features], expanding support, raising prices January 15th."

---

**SECTION 5: THE ANNUAL FORMULA & LIFETIME VALUE (8:30–9:30)**

Most SaaS uses this annual formula:

Monthly price times 12 times 0.8 equals annual price.

So $49 per month equals $588 per year normally, but the annual offer is $470. That's a 20% discount. This is simple and feels fair.

Here's the data point everyone misses:

From indie SaaS benchmarks: Average customer pays $34-47 per month. Average churn is 5-7% per month. Average lifetime value is 12-18 months.

This means: $39 price point times 15 month average lifespan equals $585 lifetime value per customer.

If you spend $100 acquiring a customer, you make $485 profit.

If you get 10 customers from your network—free acquisition—you make $4,850 profit.

This is how indie products become profitable so fast. Low acquisition costs plus reasonable lifetime value equals sustainable business.
