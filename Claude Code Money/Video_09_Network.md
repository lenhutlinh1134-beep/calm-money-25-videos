**SECTION 1: THE FOUR RELATIONSHIP TYPES (0:30–2:45)**

The best sales technique doesn't feel like sales. It feels like: "Oh, I met someone online who helped me solve a problem. They built a product to solve it. I bought it."

That's network effect in action.

Here's the data: Developers who build genuine networks close 3-4x more deals with 1/3 the effort of traditional sales.

There are 4 types of relationships that bring customers.

Type 1: Referral partners—other service providers who are NOT your competitors.

Examples for a Claude Code developer:
- Designer who needs a developer to code her designs
- Marketing consultant who needs automation tools for clients
- HR consultant who needs internal tools
- Accountant who needs custom reporting software

How to find them: Join industry communities. Look for people selling services in adjacent spaces. Comment on their content. Reach out with genuine interest.

The outreach: "Hey [Name], I noticed you help [specific thing]. I build [specific thing]. I often see overlap with what you do. Would love to chat about potential collaboration."

What happens: 10% might become a client. 60% might refer you to clients. 30% might become a partner—they refer you, you refer them.

This is the highest ROI activity. One partner can send you 2-3 customers per month.

How many partners do you need? 5-10. That's 10-30 customers per month from one activity.

Type 2: Content audience—people reading or watching your stuff.

Best formats: LinkedIn posts 2-3x per week. Tech blog posts 1-2x per month. YouTube channel. Twitter/X daily when consistent.

Content that converts: Case studies. Process breakdowns. Industry insights. Lessons learned.

This isn't direct selling. It's proving you know your shit.

Timeline: Month 1-3, build audience 20-50 people following. Month 4-6, audience grows to 100-500 people. Month 7-12, audience engagement—people asking you for work.

By month 8-12, you get 1-2 customers per month from content audience just reaching out.

Investment: 5-10 hours per week of writing.

Type 3: Community insiders—be active and helpful where your customers hang out.

For developers: Dev.to, ProductHunt, GitHub Discussions. Indie Hackers if building products. Reddit r/webdev, r/programming. Slack communities. Twitter/X developer communities.

The play: Answer questions 2-3x per week. Share useful tools 1-2x per week. Engage genuinely. Never sell directly.

What happens: People notice you're helpful. They follow you. When they need help, they reach out. 5-10% convert to customers.

Community building is slow but solid. By month 6-12, you have 1-3 customers per month from community presence.

Type 4: Online friends—find people in your industry working on interesting things.

How: Follow 20-30 people in your industry on Twitter, LinkedIn, blogs. Engage with their content—thoughtful comments, retweets. After 3-4 months of genuine engagement, send them a message. Chat about your work, see if there's overlap.

Result: They might buy from you (15%). Refer someone (40%). Partner with you (30%). Become long-term friend (always valuable).

This is slow relationship building, but it feels authentic and converts.

---

**SECTION 2: THE COMPOUND EFFECT & NETWORK MATH (2:45–5:15)**

By month 12, you have:

Referral partners: 8 people, referring 2 customers per month each equals 16 customers.

Content audience: 500 people following, 1-2 convert equals 2 customers.

Community presence: 50-100 people know you, 2-3 refer equals 3 customers.

Online friends: 30 people, 1-2 buy or refer equals 2 customers.

Total: 23 customers from network in month 12.

This number compounds. By year 2, it doubles because your network is twice as large.

Here's the math: To build a network of 50-100 strong relationships takes 5 hours per week for 12 months. That's 260 hours total.

By month 12, you're getting 20-plus customers per month from this.

Cost per customer from network: About $13 per hour. That's 260 hours divided by 20 customers.

Compare to:

Paid ads: $50-100 per customer acquired.

Sales calls: 10 hours per customer equals $150-plus in your time.

Cold email outreach: 2-3 hours per customer equals $300-plus in your time.

Network is 10-20x more efficient.

---

**SECTION 3: SYSTEMS APPROACH & SYSTEMATIC DISTRIBUTION (5:15–7:30)**

Build these in parallel, not sequentially.

Month 1:

Week 1-2: Reach out to 5 potential referral partners.

Week 2-4: Write 1 blog post, post on LinkedIn 2 times.

Month 2:

Week 1-2: Reach out to 5 more referral partners.

Week 3-4: Write 1 blog post, post on LinkedIn 2 times, answer questions in 1 community 3 times.

Month 3 and beyond: Keep all going.

Referral partner outreach: 2-3 per month.

Content: 1 blog post plus LinkedIn 2x per week.

Community: Answer questions 3x per week.

Online friends: Engage with 5-10 per week.

This is 5-8 hours per week systematically distributed.

By the end of month 12, the system compounds. You're not working harder. You're just running a flywheel that's always spinning.

---

**SECTION 4: THE MOST IMPORTANT RULE (7:30–8:45)**

You must be genuinely helpful and not expect immediate return.

If you help someone with the mindset of "I'm helping them so they'll buy from me," they'll feel it. Your network will fail.

The correct mindset: "I enjoy helping people. If it leads to business, great."

This is harder than it sounds. But it's the difference between a strong network—genuine—and a weak one—transactional.

Here's how to know you're doing it right: You get energy from helping people, not from counting leads. You engage because you're interested, not because you're fishing. You give without keeping score.

---

**SECTION 5: MEASURING & QUARTERLY REVIEW (8:45–9:30)**

Track:

Referral partners: How many? How many referrals per month?

Content followers: Growing by 50-plus per month? If not, content isn't resonating.

Community reputation: Being recognized when you show up?

Online friends: Regular interactions? Genuine relationships?

If any are flat, adjust.
