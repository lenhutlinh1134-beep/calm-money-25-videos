**Title:** "When to Go From Solo to Hiring Your First Employee (And When NOT To)"

**Hook (0-30 seconds):**
"Most developers scale wrong. They hire too early, scale too fast, and suddenly they're managing people instead of coding. I talked to three developers who scaled correctly. They didn't hire until $10K+/month, and they hired specifically for their weakness. Here's the pattern."

**Main Script (1,500-1,800 words):**

"There's a moment every developer hits: Work is overwhelming. You're at $8K-$12K/month. You have more business than you can handle.

Then you ask: Should I hire someone?

The answer is: Maybe not.

Let me break down when you actually need to hire and what hiring gets wrong.

**THE FINANCIAL REALITY OF HIRING**

A junior developer or contractor costs you:
- Part-time (20 hours/week): $1,500-$2,500/month
- Full-time (40 hours/week): $3,000-$5,000/month

This might reduce your personal workload by 30-50%. But it cuts your profit in half.

Example:
- Revenue: $10K/month
- Your labor (before hiring): $10K profit
- Revenue: $10K/month with new hire at $2,500/month
- Your profit: $7.5K

Did you gain freedom? Yes. Did you gain money? No.

Most developers hire thinking they'll grow revenue. They don't. Revenue stays flat for 3-6 months while they train the new person.

**WHEN TO HIRE (The Real Signals)**

Signal 1: Turning away clients
'I have 3 clients wanting to work with me, but I don't have time. I'm literally losing revenue because I'm overbooked.'

This is the signal. You're not maxing capacity. You're leaving money on the table.

Hiring here: New person takes overflow. You now do $15K instead of $10K. New person costs $2,500. Net gain: $2,500.

Signal 2: You hate part of the job
'I love coding. I hate sales. I'm spending 20 hours on sales, 20 hours coding.'

If you hire a sales person to take sales completely off your plate, you can code 40 hours. Does that generate $3,000+ in extra revenue? Yes.

Here, hiring makes sense.

Signal 3: You want to scale a product
'I have a SaaS making $2K/month. To grow it to $10K/month, I need dedicated marketing and feature development.'

Hiring a marketer here: Makes sense if product revenue is currently constrained by marketing, not product quality.

**WHEN NOT TO HIRE (The Wrong Reasons)**

Wrong reason 1: 'I'm tired'
If you're tired, the solution is boundaries, not hiring.

Set a limit: 'I work max 40 hours/week.' Protect that time. Stop taking projects over that.

If you can't say no to clients, hiring won't help. You'll just be busy AND managing someone.

Wrong reason 2: 'I think hiring will make me feel like a "real business"'
This is ego. Ignore it.

Hiring is a tool for growth, not validation.

Wrong reason 3: 'Everyone else is hiring'
This is FOMO. Other developers might be burning cash hiring too early.

Focus on your unit economics. If hiring doesn't increase profit, don't do it.

**THE HIRING DECISION FRAMEWORK**

Ask these three questions:

Q1: Will this hire increase revenue by at least 3x their salary?
- If yes: Hire.
- If no: Don't.

Example: Hiring a developer at $3K/month needs to add $10K+/month in revenue. Does delegating projects do that? Yes. Hire.

Q2: Do I have proven systems they can follow?
- If yes: Hire.
- If no: Build systems first, then hire.

Example: If you don't have a documented project process, hiring will be chaos.

Q3: Can I afford a 3-month ramp where they produce 0 revenue?
- If yes: Hire.
- If no: Grow first, then hire.

Example: New hire takes 3 months to be productive. Can you afford that? If your buffer is less than $5K, probably not.

If all three are YES: Hire.
If any is NO: Don't hire yet.

**THE FIRST HIRE STRATEGICALLY**

Most developers hire for the role they hate most:
- Sales/operations person (so they can code)
- Developer (so they can take more projects)
- Marketer (so they can build product)

The strategic first hire: Someone who does what you hate most AND what generates the most revenue.

Examples:
- If you hate sales but sales generates revenue: Hire sales first
- If you hate coding but coding generates revenue: Hire developer first
- If you love both but operations is killing you: Hire operations first

**THE SALARY STRUCTURE**

Don't hire full-time at first. Hire contract/part-time.

Why:
- Lower risk if it doesn't work out
- Easier to scale up (add hours) than scale down (fire)
- Better for the candidate (they can test fit)

Structure:
- Month 1: 10 hours/week at $25/hour ($250/week)
- Month 2-3: 10-15 hours/week
- Month 4+: Re-evaluate. Full-time or stay part-time?

This de-risks the relationship.

**THE ONBOARDING THAT WORKS**

Most hiring fails because onboarding is bad.

Successful onboarding:
- Week 1: Shadow you on real projects (they watch, learn your process)
- Week 2-3: Do simple tasks with feedback (you review everything)
- Week 4+: Do tasks with minimal feedback

Time investment from you: 40-60 hours over 4 weeks.

This is hard. But doing it correctly saves you 100+ hours later.

**ALTERNATIVE TO HIRING: OUTSOURCING**

Before you hire, consider outsourcing:

- Specific tasks (testing, deployment, documentation): Upwork contractor ($500-1K/month)
- Specific services (copywriting, design): Freelancer ($1K-3K/month)
- Specific functions (bookkeeping, taxes): Virtual assistant ($500-1K/month)

Cost is similar, but you don't manage people.

Many successful developers outsource instead of hiring. It's totally valid.

**THE DECISION TREE**

1. Are you turning away clients due to capacity?
   - Yes → Hire a developer or contractor. You'll grow revenue.
   - No → Skip this.

2. Is there a task you hate that takes 15+ hours/week?
   - Yes → Outsource that task first. Hire person if needed.
   - No → Skip this.

3. Are you at $15K+/month and want to scale further?
   - Yes → Hire for scaling. Plan to go $30K+/month.
   - No → Skip this.

4. Do you have working systems documented?
   - Yes → Ready to hire.
   - No → Document systems first.

**THE PROFIT REALITY**

Most developers reach $15K/month solo.

Beyond $15K, it gets hard without help.

At $25K/month with one hire, you typically make:
- Revenue: $25K
- Hire salary: $3K-5K
- Tools, taxes, etc: $3K
- Your take-home: $17K-$19K

This is better than $10K/month solo because you're scaling.

But it also comes with management overhead.

**YOUR DECISION**

If you're at $8K-$12K/month:
1. Are you turning away revenue? (If yes, hiring makes sense)
2. Can you document your process? (If no, don't hire yet)
3. Do you want to manage people? (If no, outsource instead)

Answer these honestly. That determines your next move."

**Key Data Points:**
1. Most developer hires don't increase revenue in first 6 months (2025 survey)
2. Successful hires require 40-60 hours of onboarding investment
3. Part-time contractors are 30% more successful first hires than full-time employees
