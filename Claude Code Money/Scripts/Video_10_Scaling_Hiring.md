## PHẦN 2 — VOICEOVER SCRIPT

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 1:00]**

Most developers scale wrong. They hire too early and watch their profit disappear.

I talked to three developers who scaled correctly. They all waited for the same signal: They were literally turning away clients.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: If you're making $10K/month and you hire someone at $3K/month, your profit goes DOWN, not up. At least for the first 3-6 months.

So in this video, I'm going to show you exactly when hiring makes sense, when it doesn't, and how to structure your first hire so you don't destroy profit.

**[THE FINANCIAL REALITY — 1:00 to 2:15]**

Let me give you the math.

A contractor costs you $1,500-$2,500 per month for part-time, or $3,000-$5,000 for full-time.

This might reduce your workload by 30-50%. But look at the profit.

Before hire: $10K revenue, zero costs, $10K profit.

After hire: $10K revenue, $3K contractor cost, $7K profit.

You lost $3K in profit to free up time.

The only way hiring works is if that freed-up time generates MORE revenue. At least $10K more. So your new revenue is $20K, costs are $3K, profit is $17K.

Then you've grown.

Most developers don't see that revenue increase. They just get tired and hire. Then revenue stays flat and profit drops.

**[WHEN NOT TO HIRE — 2:15 to 3:45]**

Wrong reason 1: 'I'm tired.'

If you're tired, boundaries fix it, not hiring. Set a limit: 40 hours/week max. Protect that time. Stop taking more projects.

If you can't say no to clients, hiring doesn't help. You'll just be managing people while being tired.

Wrong reason 2: 'I think hiring makes me feel like a real business.'

This is ego. Ignore it.

Wrong reason 3: 'Everyone else is hiring.'

That's FOMO. Other developers might be burning cash and failing.

Focus on unit economics. If hiring doesn't increase profit, don't do it.

**[WHEN TO HIRE — THE REAL SIGNALS — 3:45 to 5:00]**

Signal 1: You're turning away clients.

'I have 3 clients wanting to work with me, but I don't have capacity. I'm literally losing revenue because I'm booked.'

This is the signal. You're not maxing capacity. You're leaving money on the table.

If you hire here: New person takes overflow. You now do $15K instead of $10K. New person costs $2,500. Net gain: $2,500 profit.

Signal 2: You hate part of the job.

'I love coding. I hate sales. I'm spending 20 hours on sales, 20 hours coding.'

If you hire a sales person to take sales completely off your plate, can you do 40 hours coding? Does that generate $3,000+ in extra revenue?

If yes: Hire.

Signal 3: You want to scale a product.

'I have a SaaS at $2K/month. To grow to $10K/month, I need dedicated marketing and features.'

Hiring a marketer makes sense if product revenue is constrained by marketing, not by product quality.

**[THE HIRING DECISION FRAMEWORK — 5:00 to 6:30]**

Ask three questions:

Q1: Will this hire increase revenue by at least 3x their salary?

If you're hiring at $3K/month, they need to add $10K+ revenue.

Does delegating projects do that? If yes: Hire.

Q2: Do I have proven systems they can follow?

If you don't have a documented process, hiring will be chaos.

Build systems first. Then hire.

Q3: Can I afford 3 months where they produce zero revenue?

New hires take 3 months to be productive. Can you afford that?

If all three are YES: Hire.

Any NO: Don't hire yet.

**[STRATEGIC FIRST HIRE — 6:30 to 7:45]**

Most developers hire for the role they hate most.

But the strategic first hire is: Someone who does what you hate AND generates the most revenue.

Examples:

If you hate sales but sales generates revenue: Hire sales first.

If you hate coding but coding generates revenue: Hire developer first.

If you love both but operations is killing you: Hire operations first.

The key: Don't hire to make yourself happy. Hire to grow revenue.

**[CONTRACTOR VS EMPLOYEE — 7:45 to 8:45]**

Don't hire full-time at first. Hire contract/part-time.

Why: Lower risk. Easier to scale up. Better for the candidate.

Start here:

Month 1-2: 10 hours/week at $25/hour. Total: $100/week, $400/month.

Month 3-4: 10-15 hours/week.

Month 5+: Re-evaluate. Full-time or stay part-time?

This de-risks the relationship.

**[ONBOARDING — 8:45 to 9:30]**

Most hiring fails because onboarding is bad.

Successful onboarding:

Week 1: Shadow you on real projects (they watch, learn your process).

Week 2-3: Do simple tasks with feedback (you review everything).

Week 4+: Do tasks with minimal feedback.

Time from you: 40-60 hours over 4 weeks.

This is hard. But doing it right saves 100+ hours later.

**[ALTERNATIVE: OUTSOURCING — 9:30 to 10:15]**

Before you hire, consider outsourcing.

Specific tasks: Testing, deployment, documentation. Cost: $500-$1K/month.

Specific functions: Copywriting, design. Cost: $1K-$3K/month.

Business functions: Bookkeeping, taxes. Cost: $500-$1K/month.

Cost is similar to hiring, but you don't manage people. Many successful developers outsource instead of hiring. It's valid.

**[PROFIT MATH AT SCALE — 10:15 to 11:00]**

Here's the math at $25K/month with one hire:

Revenue: $25K.
Hire salary: $3K-$5K.
Tools, taxes, etc: $3K.
Your take-home: $17K-$19K.

This is better than $10K solo because you're scaling.

But it comes with management overhead.

If you don't want to manage people, stay solo longer. Or outsource instead of hiring.

**[CLOSING + CTA — 11:00 to 11:30]**

The decision is simple: Are you turning away revenue?

If yes: Hire.

If no: Don't.
