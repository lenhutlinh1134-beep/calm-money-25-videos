**SECTION 1: THE FINANCIAL REALITY OF HIRING (0:30–2:15)**

There's a moment every developer hits: Work is overwhelming. You're at $8K-12K per month. You have more business than you can handle.

Then you ask: Should I hire someone?

The answer is: Maybe not.

A junior developer or contractor costs you: Part-time 20 hours per week, $1,500-2,500 per month. Full-time 40 hours per week, $3,000-5,000 per month.

This might reduce your personal workload by 30-50%. But it cuts your profit in half.

Example: Revenue $10K per month. Your labor before hiring: $10K profit. Revenue $10K per month with new hire at $2,500 per month. Your profit: $7.5K.

Did you gain freedom? Yes. Did you gain money? No.

Most developers hire thinking they'll grow revenue. They don't. Revenue stays flat for 3-6 months while they train the new person.

---

**SECTION 2: WHEN TO HIRE (THE REAL SIGNALS) (2:15–4:45)**

Signal 1: Turning away clients.

"I have 3 clients wanting to work with me, but I don't have time. I'm literally losing revenue because I'm overbooked."

This is the signal. You're not maxing capacity. You're leaving money on the table.

Hiring here: New person takes overflow. You now do $15K instead of $10K. New person costs $2,500. Net gain: $2,500.

Signal 2: You hate part of the job.

"I love coding. I hate sales. I'm spending 20 hours on sales, 20 hours coding."

If you hire a sales person to take sales completely off your plate, can you code 40 hours and generate $3,000-plus in extra revenue? Yes.

Here, hiring makes sense.

Signal 3: You want to scale a product.

"I have a SaaS making $2K per month. To grow it to $10K per month, I need dedicated marketing and feature development."

Hiring a marketer here makes sense if product revenue is currently constrained by marketing, not product quality.

---

**SECTION 3: WHEN NOT TO HIRE (WRONG REASONS) (4:45–6:15)**

Wrong reason 1: I'm tired.

If you're tired, the solution is boundaries, not hiring.

Set a limit: "I work max 40 hours per week." Protect that time. Stop taking projects over that.

If you can't say no to clients, hiring won't help. You'll just be busy and managing someone.

Wrong reason 2: I think hiring will make me feel like a "real business."

This is ego. Ignore it. Hiring is a tool for growth, not validation.

Wrong reason 3: Everyone else is hiring.

This is FOMO. Other developers might be burning cash hiring too early.

Focus on your unit economics. If hiring doesn't increase profit, don't do it.

---

**SECTION 4: THE HIRING DECISION FRAMEWORK & FIRST HIRE (6:15–8:30)**

Ask three questions:

Q1: Will this hire increase revenue by at least 3x their salary?

If yes: Hire.

If no: Don't.

Example: Hiring a developer at $3K per month needs to add $10K-plus per month in revenue. Does delegating projects do that? Yes. Hire.

Q2: Do I have proven systems they can follow?

If yes: Hire.

If no: Build systems first, then hire.

Example: If you don't have a documented project process, hiring will be chaos.

Q3: Can I afford a 3-month ramp where they produce 0 revenue?

If yes: Hire.

If no: Grow first, then hire.

Example: New hire takes 3 months to be productive. Can you afford that? If your buffer is less than $5K, probably not.

If all three are YES: Hire.

If any is NO: Don't hire yet.

Now, the first hire strategically: Hire for the role you hate most AND what generates the most revenue.

Examples: If you hate sales but sales generates revenue, hire sales first. If you hate coding but coding generates revenue, hire developer first. If you love both but operations is killing you, hire operations first.

For salary structure: Don't hire full-time at first. Hire contract or part-time.

Why: Lower risk if it doesn't work out. Easier to scale up—add hours—than scale down—fire. Better for the candidate—they can test fit.

Structure: Month 1, 10 hours per week at $25 per hour ($250 per week). Month 2-3, 10-15 hours per week. Month 4 and beyond, re-evaluate. Full-time or stay part-time?

This de-risks the relationship.

---

**SECTION 5: ONBOARDING & ALTERNATIVES (8:30–9:30)**

Most hiring fails because onboarding is bad.

Successful onboarding: Week 1, shadow you on real projects—they watch, learn your process. Week 2-3, do simple tasks with feedback—you review everything. Week 4-plus, do tasks with minimal feedback.

Time investment from you: 40-60 hours over 4 weeks.

This is hard. But doing it correctly saves you 100-plus hours later.

Alternative to hiring: Outsourcing.

Before you hire, consider outsourcing:

Specific tasks like testing, deployment, documentation: Upwork contractor $500-1K per month.

Specific services like copywriting, design: Freelancer $1K-3K per month.

Specific functions like bookkeeping, taxes: Virtual assistant $500-1K per month.

Cost is similar, but you don't manage people.

Many successful developers outsource instead of hiring. It's totally valid.

3. Do you want to manage people?

Answer these honestly. That determines your next move.
